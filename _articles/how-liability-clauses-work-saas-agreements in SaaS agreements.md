When a business enters into a Software as a Service (SaaS) agreement, whether as a customer or a founder issuing terms to enterprise clients (B2B), one of the core positions to be negotiated is the liability clause. Despite this, it is frequently the least understood. 

This article explains how liability clauses operate under Australian law, the forms they commonly take in SaaS agreements and their practical significance for both parties to a B2B transaction

## What is a Liability Clause

"Liability clause" is a commercial label that is applied to a range of related contractual provisions that aim to exclude, limit or apportion responsibility for loss or damage arising in connection with a breach of contract, negligence or other actionable conduct. In commercial agreements, they typically appear in two forms:

- A limitation of liability clause which caps the total amount one party can recover from the other, regardless of the amount of the actual loss suffered.

> "The Supplier’s total liability under this agreement is limited to the fees paid by the Customer in the 12 months before the claim arose."

- An exclusion of liability clause which removes liability for a certain category of loss. 

> "Neither party is liable for any consequential loss, including loss of profit, revenue, data, or business opportunity."

## How Australian Courts interpret these Clauses

The leading Australian authority on the interpretation of exclusion and limitation clauses is Darlington Futures Ltd v Delco Australia Pty Ltd (1986) 161 CLR 500, a decision of the High Court of Australia. [^1] 

The High Court confirmed that exclusion and limitation clauses are to be interpreted according to their natural and ordinary meaning, read in the context of the contract as a whole.[^2] Where ambiguity remains after applying ordinary principles of contractual interpretation, the contra proferentem rule may apply, so that the clause is construed against the party seeking to rely on it.[^3] 

Australian courts will give effect to exclusion and limitation clauses according to their clear terms and will not construe them as extending beyond what is expressed in clear and unambiguous language, read in context.[^4]

## Common structures in SaaS agreements 

Liability clauses in SaaS agreements vary significantly but a number of structures appear with regularity:

### Mutual exclusion of consequential loss

Both parties agree to exclude liability for consequential loss, including loss of profit, revenue, data and goodwill. These clauses are often drafted with symmetrical wording, however the practical effect is asymmetric, with platform provider exposure to large consequential loss claims typically far greater than the customer’s.

### Aggregate cap linked to fees paid

Liability is capped at the total fees paid by the consumer in the X amount of months preceding the claim. For a customer paying $500 a month with X being 12 months, this means the maximum recoverable amount, regardless of the actual loss, would be $6,000.

### Carve-outs from the cap and exclusions

Standard form SaaS agreements (those with limited or no ability to be negotiated) typically carve out certain categories of liability from both the monetary limitation and exclusion. Common carve-outs include liability arising from:

- fraud;

- misconduct;

- confidentiality obligations; and

- intellectual property indemnities.

## Why It Matters for Founders

For founders on both sides of a SaaS agreement, the practical effect of these clauses is significant.

- A founder issuing a SaaS agreement on standard terms carries the risk that a broadly drafted exclusion or limitation clause may not operate as intended. The High Court confirmed in Darlington Futures Ltd v Delco Australia Pty Ltd (1986) 161 CLR 500 that exclusion clauses are construed according to their natural and ordinary meaning in context, meaning a clause drafted to exclude all liability may be read narrowly if the circumstances giving rise to the loss fall outside that meaning.

- A founder signing a SaaS agreement as a customer should understand that an aggregate cap linked to fees paid may leave them with limited recourse in the event of a significant platform failure, data loss, or service disruption. regardless of the actual loss suffered.

- Both parties to a SaaS agreement should be aware that the agreement, subject to it being classified as a standard form contract, may be subject to the unfair contract terms regime under the Australian Consumer Law (Schedule 2, Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth)). From 9 November 2023, proposing or relying on unfair terms in standard form contracts attracts civil penalties. 

## What This Means in Practice

Liability clauses in SaaS agreements are not boilerplate. The interaction between how courts construe exclusion clauses, the cap structures commonly used in the market, and the regulatory framework governing standard form contracts means these provisions carry real legal weight in both directions.

Understanding how these clauses are structured and what legal framework applies to them is the starting point for any informed commercial conversation about a SaaS agreement.

For advice specific to your circumstances, consult a qualified Australian legal practitioner.
