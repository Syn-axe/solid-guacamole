## Introduction

Many businesses assume that drafting a contractor agreement and labelling a worker as a "contractor", "consultant" or "service provider" is enough to avoid employment obligations. However, Australian Courts have made it clear that a worker's legal status depends on the rights and obligations created by the relationship, not simply the label used by the parties.

## What is the difference between an employee and a contractor?

The difference between an employee and a contractor can be summarised as follows:

An employee works as a representative of a business and performs work under the direction and control of the employer.

An independent contractor operates their own business and provides services as a separate commercial party.

The distinction is not determined simply by the label used in an agreement. Courts assess the legal relationship created by the rights and obligations between the parties.

## How do Australian courts determine whether someone is an employee or contractor?

Australian Courts focus on the substance of the relationship rather than the label applied by the parties. The High Court of Australia has explained that an employee works as part of another party's business, whereas an independent contractor performs work as a principal in their own right. [^1]

As the High Court observed in *CFMMEU v Personnel Contracting Pty Ltd* [2022] HCA 1:

> "an independent contractor carries out his work, not as a representative but as a principal."[^2]

Accordingly, describing a worker as a "contractor", "consultant" or "service provider" does not determine their legal status. While the wording used by the parties may provide some context, courts examine the rights and obligations created by the agreement to determine the true nature of the relationship.[^3]

## What are the key indicators of an employee or contractor relationship?

Historically, Australian Courts considered a range of factors when assessing employment status, including control, delegation, commercial risk and the method of payment.[^4] However, the High Court of Australia has clarified that where the parties have a comprehensive written agreement, the focus is primarily on the rights and obligations created by that contract.[^5]

In practice, businesses should consider three key questions when engaging contractors:

**Control**

Does the business control how, when and where the work is performed or does the worker operate independently?

**Independence**

Does the worker provide services as an independent business, including managing their own time, process, risks and commercial arrangements?

**Commercial risk**

Is the worker responsible for delivering the result and bearing the costs of their business or is the business responsible for those risks?[^6]

## Why does this matter for business owners?

Getting the distinction between an employee and contractor wrong can create unexpected costs and compliance issues for a business.

Where a worker is incorrectly treated as an independent contractor, the business may face obligations it did not anticipate, including potential liability for unpaid superannuation, taxation obligations, leave entitlements and penalties.[^7]

For growing businesses, the risk is often highest when a contractor becomes a long-term, essential part of operations and the practical reality of the relationship begins to resemble employment. Understanding the distinction early allows founders to make more informed decisions about how they structure working relationships as their business grows.

## What does this mean in practice?

A contractor agreement does not create a contractor relationship by itself. The agreement must reflect a genuine independent business relationship, supported by the actual rights and obligations between the parties.
